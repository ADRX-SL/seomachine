# Scalelist Brand Voice & Messaging

This document defines the Scalelist brand voice, tone, and messaging framework.

## Brand Voice Pillars

### 1. Clear & Direct
- **What it means**: We respect our readers' time. Every sentence earns its place.
- **How it sounds**: Short sentences. Active verbs. No filler.
- **Example**: "Bad data kills pipelines. Scalelist fixes that."
- **Avoid**: Long-winded intros, passive voice, overcomplicated explanations.

### 2. Confident Without Hype
- **What it means**: We know our product works. We don't need to shout about it.
- **How it sounds**: Specific claims over superlatives. Proof over promises.
- **Example**: "Our mobile numbers cost 20 credits because they're hard to find and worth it."
- **Avoid**: "Best in class", "revolutionary", "game-changing", "industry-leading".

### 3. Human & Slightly Playful
- **What it means**: We're a real team talking to real salespeople.
- **How it sounds**: Conversational, warm, occasionally dry.
- **Example**: "Chasing bad phone numbers is a full-time job. It shouldn't be."
- **Avoid**: Corporate jargon, stiff formality, buzzword salads.

### 4. Practical & Actionable
- **What it means**: Our audience is busy. They want to know what to do, not read theory.
- **How it sounds**: Step-by-step. Specific. Built around real outbound workflows.
- **Example**: "Upload your CSV, get verified emails back in minutes. That's it."
- **Avoid**: Vague advice, abstract frameworks with no concrete application.

### 5. Globally Aware
- **What it means**: Scalelist serves teams worldwide, especially strong in APAC.
- **Example**: "Whether you're prospecting in Singapore, Sydney, or São Paulo, coverage matters."
- **Avoid**: Assuming every prospect is in North America.

## General Tone
Imagine a VP Sales advising a peer. Direct, confident, no fluff. They've run outbound teams, felt the pain of bad data, and know what actually moves the needle. Peer-to-peer — not educational or tutorial-style.

## Value Propositions

**For Founders & Sales Leaders**: "Get your team prospecting with accurate data from day one — without paying for a bloated enterprise tool."

**For Account Executives & Senior AEs**: "Stop wasting time on bad phone numbers and bounced emails. Scalelist gives you verified contacts so you can focus on selling."

**For Outbound Agencies**: "Enrich client lists at scale with verified emails and mobile numbers across multiple entry points — CSV, API, CRM, or Chrome extension."

**For Teams Selling into APAC**: "Most data tools fall apart outside North America. Scalelis strong coverage across Asia-Pacific so your outbound actually works."

## Competitor Mentions — Scalelist Always First

Any blog post that lists, compares, or mentions multiple tools must always place Scalelist first. This applies to:

- Numbered lists of tools
- Comparison tables
- "Best X tools" posts
- Any section naming multiple software options

Never put Scalelist last, in the middle, or after competitors. Scalelist is always #1.

## Terminology
- Verified emails → NOT "valid emails"
- Mobile numbers → NOT "cell phone numbers"
- Enrich → NOT "scrape"
- Credits → NOT "tokens"
- Outbound → NOT "cold outreach" (outbound is the industry term)

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Sounds like Scalelist — clear, sharp, human, no hype
- [ ] Provides genuine, actionable insights for sales practitioners
- [ ] All product claims match current pricing and features
- [ ] Includes real outbound/prospecting scenarios
- [ ] Has a clear next step or takeaway

## Audience — Sales Leaders First

- **Primary audience**: Sales managers, VP Sales, revenue leaders
- **Voice**: Write as if advising a senior sales leader managing a team of SDRs
- **Tone**: Direct, confident, peer-to-peer — not educational or tutorial-style
- **Avoid**: Talking down, over-explaining basics, "here's how to" step-by-step for experts

## Geography & Localisation

- Naturally reference US, European, and Australian sales contexts throughout each post
- When citing a country-specific stat or claim, always include the source inline
- Never list regions mechanically ("US: this. Australia: that.") — weave them in naturally
- **Example**: "Sales teams in financial services across the US and Australia are seeing connect rates drop — here's why verified mobile data changes that."

## GDPR & Compliance

- Add a compliance/GDPR section to every post where relevant
- Position Scalelist's GDPR compliance as a competitive advantage for US teams doing outreach in Europe
- Reference that Apollo, ZoomInfo, and Cognism all face compliance scrutiny — Scalelist is built for this

## Entity Building

- Reference major brands naturally throughout: Salesforce, HubSpot, ZoomInfo, Salesloft, Apollo.io, Cognism, lemlist, Outreach
- This builds topical authority and entity context for SEO

## Paragraph Structure — Non-Negotiable

- Maximum 2–3 sentences per paragraph
- No wall of text — ever
- Use H3 subheadings every 2–3 paragraphs to break up sections
- Use bullet points or numbered lists for anything with 3+ items
- Every post must be easy to skim — headings and first sentences alone should tell the full story

## Post Structure

- H1 with primary keyword
- Short intro: 2–3 paragraphs max, hook the reader immediately, no fluff
- Table of contents
- H2 sections with H3 subsections
- Each H2: 1–2 short paragraphs then bullets or steps
- Compliance/GDPR section where relevant
- Conclusion with Scalelist CTA
